Mental Simulation

Before a chess player makes a move, they imagine what happens next. "If I move the knight here, they'll take my bishop, then I'll..." This preview is quick, rough, and often wrong ‚Äî but it's enough to avoid obvious mistakes.

Engram does the same thing: before committing to an answer, it runs a quick, low-fidelity simulation ‚Äî just 2-3 diffusion steps instead of the usual 5. The result is blurry, but if even the blurry version looks wrong, the model knows to try something else.

Continuous Metacognition

Most AI systems work like a student who only checks their answer when they hand in the exam. If they made a mistake on line 3, they won't catch it until they've built 50 lines on top of it.

Engram checks after every single step. A built-in Process Reward Model (PRM) monitors every intermediate result and asks: "Does this still make sense? Am I getting warmer or colder?" If confidence drops, it re-masks uncertain tokens and tries again.

This is "whole-board checking" ‚Äî stepping back to see the full picture, continuously, not just at the end.

The Analogy

GPT checks its work at the very end. By then it's too late.

Engram steps back and looks at the whole board after every move.
